Arcs

December 2014

Application

Arcs is a web application which demonstrates how shortest distance paths on the earth appear 'curved' on most rectangular maps. Arcs was built using a free 3d Javscript library called Phoria which itself uses HTML5's canvas library. I had to write most of the logic of displaying paths and calculating their projections. I also implemented mobile event triggers, since Phoria's mouse handling does not include this.

I thought of this project following a discussion I had with my roommate and his girlfriend. She had recently got back to Texas following a six month mission trip in India. We talked about how her flight back took her much farther north than we would have expected, because it appeared as a curve on the map.

I realized this behavior of maps was related to the natural distortions of map projections. The earth, of course, is not flat, which means that we have to make some sacrifices with geometric accuracy when we project the earth's surface onto a flat map. The Mercator Projection, for example, greatly exaggerates the sizes of areas near the north and south pole, causing Greenland to look much larger than it actually is:

Image Credit: Pratham

Other kinds of projections do preserve relative area, but at the cost of distorting angles. It is a well known mathematical result that it is impossible for a map projection to both be conformal (angle-preserving) and area-preserving. A shortest path between two points on a surface is called a Geodesic. On a plane, all Geodesics are line segments, but on a sphere, it is curved. Similarly, when Geodesics are projected, they also tend to appear curved. There are some projections where Geodesics do appear as line segments, which are called Gnomic Projections. Surprisingly, a Gnomonic Projection is actually not conformal, which is a misconception I had.

Firebot

November 2014

Executable • Github

Firebot is a game I wrote with my friend Andy Tran during the 2014 Live Lab GameJam at Texas A&M University. The objective of the game is to put out fires and fight fire themed enemies.

The main character is a floating robot that is armed with a hose and a gun. Both weapons produce a lot of recoil, which can be used in some platforming challenges. One gameplay mechanic I wanted to include was to have enemies be invulnerable when they are on fire; the player would have to extinguish them before shooting them. Unfortunately we were only able to get static fire and one enemy type into the game before the GameJam ended.

We used Unity as our game development environment. Before writing this game I had zero experience with Unity or game development platforms, or even C#, which is what Unity uses. A lot of the code for this project was re-purposed from Unity's tutorial platformer game, although very little original code remained by the end. Art assets were lazily made in MS Paint; originally the Firebot was meant to have legs.

I don't have any plans on continuing this particular project. I didn't really like how the movement and the 'feel' of the game turned out; playtesters were often frustrated with how difficult basic platforming was (although the playtesters' children loved the game, which is a point of pride for me). However, the concept might be something I revisit in the (far) future.

Numerical Monoids

July 2014

JMM Slides • Report

I participated in an REU at Sam Houston State during June and July of 2014. An REU (Research Experience for Undergraduates) is a summer program held by a university or other academic institution that is intended to introduce research to undergraduates. This is important for Mathematics majors, because most math courses don't represent math research very well at all. REUs are funded by the NSF and each REU participant is provided a pretty generous stipend. Most REUs are pretty small; mine was on the larger side with about 20 students.

During the REU I was introduced to a topic area that was friendly to undergraduates (most research areas require years of learning to get to point of being able to contribute new results; this is also known as 'graduate school'), given an open (i.e. unsolved) problem to work on, and turned loose for two months. I checked in with my advisor (a professor at Sam Houston State University) every day, but for the most part I did not get much guidance. This was both good, in that I was forced to be independent and persistent, and bad, in that it would be frustrating when I got stuck and did not get much help.

The final result of the program was a fourteen page paper. I also produced a presentation that was shown at the Joint Mathematics Meetings the following January. Unfortunately the topic area (numerical monoids) does not provide very many opportunities for graphics, nor is it easy to explain what my problem was. I don't suspect many readers of this will be interested in learning about the problem, but if you are mathematically literate, feel free to send me an email and I'd love to talk about it (although at this point it is surprising how much of my own research I've forgotten).

Raytracer

May 2014

Executable

A Raytracer is a method of rendering three dimensional objects. It does this by shooting a ray for every pixel of the image, which is supposed to simulate light particles entering the point of view from that direction. This is kind of the opposite of what traditional renderers do, which is determine what polygons are on the screen and then paint them onto pixels. The advantage of raytracers is that they much better handle things such as reflection and refraction, since these are effects that bend or redirect light rays. Raytracers can generate some very photorealistic results:

Image Credit: Gilles Tran

My raytracer was mostly made from scratch; I used OpenGL, but only to draw individual pixels onto the screen. There were three major components to the raytracer. The first thing we had to do was represent a ray, and shoot out rays for every pixel of our display port. The second task was building a library for representing shapes; we only really had to implement spheres and planes, as well as the stretching of those objects. In particular, we needed to encode the material (i.e. color behavior) of the object, as well as be able to get the intersection of any ray with that object, as well as the normal vector of that intersection. The third and final thing to do was to generate lighting data for every intersection. We had to shoot additional rays to see if the intersection point was visible from any lights, and possibly shoot more rays for light reflection or refraction.

This program was the final project of my Computer Graphics class. In the class, we learned many different graphical algorithms; there were about six different two week projects. Some of the other projects included polygon clipping and fractal generation. Most of the class focused on 2D rendering, but we started to dip our toes into 3D stuff near the end of the semester. Unfortunately we never really pushed our technologies to the point where do anything real time or interactive, but that is something I would like to learn about in the future.

The archive contains two files: an executable, and a dll fille named glut32, which is just a library file needed to run the program. When using the executable, you can press the number keys 1 through 4 to get different scenes.

Basic CPU

May 2014

Zip Archive

For the final project of my computer organization class, I had to design and implement a basic 4 function CPU. The designs of CPUs was the culmination of our curriculum: we began by learning about logic gates and worked our way through more and more complicated circuits.

Originally we were going to implement a processor that could run x86, but due to time constraints the project was simplified to something that could add two small vectors of numbers. It was still a great deal of work, and in some ways forced us to become more intimately familiar with CPU design; instead of being able to copy x86, we had to come up with our own assembly language. We implemented the CPU in a circuit simulator called Logisim.

This was a team project; my role in the project was to come up with the high level design of the circuit, as well as the commands and structure of the assembly language. The rest of the team made the real (virtual) circuit.

Freshman Year

College is a clean slate for a lot of people; it definitely was for me. My senior year of high school was not a pleasant experience; I had a lot of issues with my family and with focusing on coursework. My grades took a big hit, but luckily I was still able to get into Texas A&M.

Fortunately living away from home was a great influence; the perception that I had to be fully responsible for my own performance was a great motivator. I was someone who had the reputation of being a slacker in high school, but my work ethic really took a 180 degree turn as soon as I started college.

I applied to the Chemical Engineering school but got into Applied Mathematics, which turned out to be a wonderful outcome. I found out I really liked the advanced, proof-based math courses that were not taught in high school. I also rediscovered my love for programming and was delighted to recognize the mathematical underpinnings of the field.

    • Sam Houston State REU

    Unsure of what I wanted to do in the summer, I applied for several summer research programs in the spring of 2014. I ended up getting into an REU (Research Experience for Undergraduates) at Sam Houston State.

    The program was challenging and exciting, but surprisingly relaxed. In independent research, you are surrounded by people who have immense drive, but you don't really find as much of the traditional carrot-stick motivators in classwork (although being a professor may be different, with publications and whatnot).     Junior Year

    In my third year of college I started to more heavily focus on my computer science classes. This was not really a result of preference, but rather I realized that I needed to do this in order to fulfil my double major requirements.

    In my fall semester I worked both as a peer teacher for the CSCE department and a teaching assistant for the math department. It was great to be able to help lots of people with their classes, but ultimately it was too many hours, so I dropped my peer teaching job in the spring.

    I flirted a bit with presenting research; I presented my REU project at the Joint Mathematics Meetings in San Antonio in January, and my project in Geometric Group Theory with Dr. Grigorchuk at the Undergraduate Geometry and Topography conference at Texas university during February.

    • Charles Schwab Internship

    In the Summer of 2015, I participated in an internship at Charles Schwab. This was a nine week program that lasted from June 15 to August 14, at the new Denver campus in Lone Tree.

    Interns were recruited for many departments. I worked in the Integrated Data Warehouse (IDW), whose main purpose is to consolidate data from other internal databases so that access and usage of that data is easy and convenient.

    Another part of the intern experience was attending weekly roundtables to learn about company culture and the financial services industry in general. I learned about financial wellness, how to identify my personal strengths, and many other things in these sessions.

    Overall, my experience with the internship was extremely positive. I learned a lot, both about data warehousing as well as the financial industry in general.
